# **Executive Summary**
D5 builds the brand on top of the audience and product decisions locked in D1–D4. The brand brief is not a creative exercise; it is a strategic instrument for a regulated medical-adjacent product that must compete with the well-funded incumbents (Hello Heart, Withings, Omron, CarePredict) while reaching an audience those incumbents have ignored: the Nigerian and African diaspora caregiver, and secondarily the US Black-American family caregiver.

The conclusions in this document follow an evidence chain. Where the data points to a single direction, we recommend it. Where the data is ambiguous, we say so and present options for the founder to decide. The brand brief is grounded in five blocks of research:

1. Naming convention analysis of 100+ existing health-wearable brands, plus a parallel analysis of successful diaspora-targeted fintechs (Sendwave, LemFi, Eversend, Afriex, Grey, Send App) to determine which naming patterns travel well across geographies.
1. Competitive voice and tone analysis of the closest competitors (Hello Heart, Lark, CarePredict, Withings, Omron) — what messages they use, what emotional levers they pull, what gaps they leave.
1. Cultural research into Black American consumer trust patterns in healthcare (Office of Minority Health, PM360 imagery study, Directions Group consumer loyalty data) and Nigerian diaspora trust patterns (Nigerian fintech success cases).
1. Color, typography, and visual-language analysis from healthcare branding studies (99designs analysis of 900+ healthcare logos, Progress.com analysis of 35 healthcare websites, ThinkPod 2025 healthcare color trend research).
1. Caregiver-app messaging research synthesizing what actually converts in this category (peace of mind, dignity, reassurance, control of caregiver burden).

# **§2 Naming — Research-Backed Recommendations**
Naming is the most consequential and least reversible brand decision. This section walks through the evidence, eliminates patterns that don't work for our audience, generates candidates that meet the criteria, and recommends three for founder evaluation.
## **2.1 Naming Patterns That Work in Health Wearables**
Analysis of 100+ wearable companies across House of Who's brand-naming review, Smart Branding's catalog, and TechCrunch coverage of recent fundings reveals four dominant naming patterns:

|**Pattern**|**Examples**|**Strengths**|**Fit for Us**|
| :- | :- | :- | :-: |
|**Evocative / Mythic**|Oura, Hera, Cala, Bellabeat, Kernel, Empatica|Memorable; suggests deeper meaning; trademark-protectable; works across cultures|**HIGH**|
|**Functional / Descriptive**|HeartGuide, BP Doctor, Fitbit, ScanWatch|Easy to understand; SEO friendly|**LOW**|
|**Compound / Synthetic**|Withings, Whoop, Ultrahuman, CarePredict|Distinctive; trademark-strong|MEDIUM|
|**Corporate / Founder**|Garmin, Omron, Philips, Medtronic|Heritage signal; trust by association|**LOW**|
### **Why Functional / Descriptive Naming is Wrong For Us**
Names like "BP Doctor" or "HeartGuide" describe what the product does — and that is exactly the problem. They commodify the product into the same category as 50 generic Amazon listings. They also create regulatory risk (per D3): names that imply diagnostic capability invite FDA scrutiny that an FDA-listed (not FDA-approved-for-diagnosis) device cannot withstand. The competitive landscape (D1) shows these descriptive names race to the bottom on price.
### **Why Evocative / Mythic Names Win**
Three of the most successful health wearables of the past decade used evocative names: Oura (from Finnish 'ourasti' meaning courageously), Whoop (a syllable signaling action), Cala (Spanish for 'cove' — calm). Per House of Who's brand naming review: "By taking into consideration the deeply human ways that we are increasingly connecting to life and then showcasing this through suggestive and evocative brand names, wearable technology is winning over curious and experimental audiences." Evocative names have three advantages our category needs: (a) they suggest the product is more than its commodity function, (b) they build trademark moat, and (c) they travel across cultures without fragmenting.
## **2.2 Naming Patterns from Successful Diaspora-Targeted Brands**
An important counter-data-point. The most successful Nigerian-diaspora-targeted brands of the past five years did NOT use Yoruba, Igbo, or Hausa words. They used English-language, action-oriented, or evocative names:

|**Brand**|**Pattern**|**Why It Worked**|
| :- | :- | :- |
|**Sendwave**|Verb + descriptor|Action-clear, English, scales beyond Nigeria. 5M+ downloads.|
|**LemFi**|Compound / synthetic|Originally 'Lemonade Finance' — rebranded to LemFi for international scale. 1M+ users; $1B+/month volume.|
|**Eversend**|Compound / English|Expanded from Uganda to Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Rwanda. Suggests 'always sending'.|
|**Grey**|English / minimal|Y Combinator 2022. 1M+ users. Designed to be culturally neutral while serving Nigerian diaspora — explicitly so it could expand into Asian diaspora later.|
|**Afriex**|Compound / 'Afri' + 'X'|'X' for exchange. Pan-African signal without being Nigerian-exclusive.|
|**Chipper Cash**|English idiom|Reached $2B unicorn status. 'Chipper' = pleasant; 'Cash' = function. Explicit cross-continent appeal.|
|**Moniepoint**|English / portmanteau|Nigeria's biggest fintech ($110M raised). 'Money' + 'Point' (terminal). Rebranded from 'TeamApt' to a more consumer-readable English compound when shifting to retail.|
|<p>**WHY THIS DATA MATTERS**</p><p>Every successful Nigerian-diaspora-targeted brand of the past five years used English-language, scalable, evocative names — NOT Yoruba/Igbo/Hausa words. The reason is structural: the diaspora itself is multilingual. A Yoruba name alienates Igbo and Hausa Nigerians. An English-language evocative name welcomes them all, and also welcomes the secondary US Black-American audience and the tertiary self-buyer audience without translation. We will follow this proven path. Cultural authenticity will be expressed in voice, imagery, founder story, and Nigerian-specific creative variants — NOT in the brand name itself.</p>|||


## **2.3 Naming Criteria**
Synthesizing all the above, the brand name we choose must pass the following criteria. Any candidate failing two or more is eliminated.

1. Pronounceable in English by anyone — Nigerian, American, British. Three syllables maximum.
1. Suggests warmth, watching-over, or care without being literal. (Eliminates: 'BPMonitor', 'CareWatch', 'HeartTracker'.)
1. Available as a .com domain, or with a clean alternative (.co, .health, .app) if .com is taken.
1. Available on USPTO for Class 9 (mobile applications) and Class 10 (medical devices) — must be checked via USPTO TESS.
1. Available on Apple App Store and Google Play Store as a unique app name.
1. No conflicting prior use in healthcare, especially not pharmaceuticals or medical devices.
1. Safe — does not have a negative meaning or unfortunate phonetic equivalent in Yoruba, Igbo, Hausa, Spanish, French, or other languages our audience speaks.
1. Trademark-strong — distinctive enough to be defensible (excludes generic English words like 'Pulse', 'Heart', 'Care' alone).
1. Evocative without being culturally narrow.
1. Works as a verb form in conversational copy — 'I check [name] every morning' should sound natural.

# **§3 Voice and Tone**
Voice is who the brand is. Tone is how the brand speaks in different moments. Both must be defined — research-grounded — before a single word of UX copy or marketing creative is written.
## **3.1 Competitive Voice Analysis**
Examination of how the closest competitors talk to users reveals where they each sit on key voice dimensions. We deliberately analyze five direct or adjacent competitors to find both the saturated zones (avoid) and the white space (occupy).

|**Brand**|**Voice Position**|**Sample Headline**|**Why It Works / Doesn't**|
| :- | :- | :- | :- |
|**Hello Heart**|Optimistic + clinical|"Take amazing care of your heart."|Speaks TO the patient. Friendly but US-corporate. Designed for B2B benefits packages.|
|**Withings**|Premium-utilitarian|"Health, made simple."|Cool, French design sensibility. Speaks to affluent quantified-self adopter. No emotional warmth.|

|**CarePredict**|Reassuring + AI-credible|"Know that Mom is ok." / "Your voice on their wrist."|This is the voice closest to where we want to sit. But CarePredict speaks to a US-suburban senior-living buyer, not diaspora.|

## **3.2 Voice Position — Where We Sit**
Mapping the competitors on two axes — Warmth (functional ↔ emotional) and Cultural Specificity (universal ↔ culturally-rooted) — reveals the white space we occupy.

|<p>**OUR VOICE COORDINATES**</p><p>HIGH WARMTH × CULTURALLY-ROOTED. None of the existing players sit here. Hello Heart, Withings, and YHE are universal-functional. CarePredict and Aloe Care are warm-but-American-suburban. We are warm AND speak with the cultural grammar of the diaspora — without being narrow. This is the voice no one else can credibly write because no one else has the founder's authentic provenance combined with the technical capability to ship the product.</p>|
| :- |
## **3.3 Brand Voice Pillars**
Four pillars define the voice. Every piece of copy — UX microcopy, marketing creative, app push notifications, AI assistant responses, App Store description, support emails — should pass all four.
### **Pillar 1: WARM**
We sound like a thoughtful family member, not a clinic. We use 'we' and 'you' liberally. We never use third-person clinical phrasing ('the patient', 'the user'). We say 'your mother', not 'the wearer'. We say 'check on her' not 'monitor her vitals'.

- **✗ AVOID:** 'Patient hypertension monitoring detected an anomaly.'
- **✓ INSTEAD:** 'Your mom's blood pressure was higher than usual today. Here's what you might want to know.'
### **Pillar 2: CALM**
We never raise our voice. Never urgent unless something genuinely urgent is happening (and even then: clear, never panicky). Caregiver users live in a low-grade state of anxiety; per the AARP family caregiving research and Billings Gazette caregiver-mental-health analysis, caregivers experience higher rates of anxiety than non-caregivers. We are the antidote to anxiety, not a contributor.

- **✗ AVOID:** '⚠️ ALERT: BP CRITICALLY HIGH ⚠️'
- **✓ INSTEAD:** 'Mom's reading is higher than her usual. We're suggesting she rest for 15 minutes and try again. Here's why this can happen.'
### **Pillar 3: PROACTIVE**
We don't wait for the caregiver to check the app. We tell them what they need to know, when they need to know it. AI summaries arrive weekly. Anomalies surface immediately. Patterns are explained, not just shown. We respect the caregiver's time and their busy life.

- **✗ AVOID:** Just showing graphs and expecting the caregiver to interpret.
- **✓ INSTEAD:** 'This week, Mom's BP averaged 138/86 — a little higher than the past month (133/82). Three of her morning readings happened before her medication time. You might want to check whether her pill schedule moved.'
### **Pillar 4: DIGNIFIED**
We never patronize either the caregiver or the parent. We never say 'simple' or 'easy' (those imply the user is being condescended to). We never use baby-talk for elders. We respect the parent's autonomy: the watch reports to the family, but the parent is in control of their own life and their own readings.

- **✗ AVOID:** 'Simple. Easy. Just for you!' (sounds infantilizing)
- **✓ INSTEAD:** 'Designed to fit your morning. Whether it's 6:00am tea or 9:00am coffee, Kena adapts to your rhythm.'
## **3.4 Voice Anti-Patterns**
Things we never do. These come up repeatedly in healthcare app copy and we will not repeat them.

- **Never use "loved one" as the primary referent.** It is generic and emotionally-flat (and overused by every caregiver-app marketer). Use specifics: 'your mom', 'your dad', 'your father-in-law', 'your aunt'. Even in copy where the relationship is unknown, prefer 'your parent' over 'your loved one'.
- **Never use "smartwatch" as the primary product noun.** We are a caregiver platform that ships with a watch. The watch is incidental. Use 'the wristband', 'the device', or just 'Kena' (when verb-able).
- **Never use medical claim language.** Per D3: words like 'diagnose', 'predict stroke', 'detect heart attack', 'cure', 'treat' are FDA enforcement triggers. Replace with 'monitor', 'track', 'help you understand', 'flag for review with a doctor', 'share with your physician'.
- **Never sound urgent unless an actual urgent situation exists.** Push notifications are not opportunities to scare the caregiver into engagement. The brand promise is REASSURANCE, not anxiety farming. (This is also a key differentiator vs. every typical health app, which pushes the user with anxious nudges.)
- **Never use Nigerian slang or Pidgin in primary brand copy.** Save these for specifically-targeted Nigerian creative variants. The primary brand is multilingual-elegant. Cultural specificity goes in CREATIVE, not in CORE COPY.
- **Never paywall a basic reading.** If a caregiver needs to know whether Mom's BP was 142/91 this morning and we hide it behind a subscription wall, we have failed the brand promise. The free tier always shows the most recent reading and the past 7 days.
- **Never make the parent feel surveilled.** Parent-side copy should emphasize agency: 'Your readings,' not 'Family is watching.' Parent should know their family sees readings (and consent to it during onboarding) but the experience should be one of partnership, not panopticon.
## **3.5 Tone Variations**
Voice is consistent. Tone shifts by context. Three principal tones, each with sample copy:
### **Tone A: Reassuring (default for everyday)**
When everything is fine — daily summaries, weekly reports, normal readings, routine syncs.

|<p>**EXAMPLE**</p><p>Good morning. Mom's blood pressure was 124/79 this morning — right where she usually is. She slept 7 hours and 12 minutes, a little more than her average. Have a good day.</p>|
| :- |
### **Tone B: Informative (for context, education, AI insights)**
When the caregiver needs to understand something — pattern explanations, what a reading means, lifestyle context.

|<p>**EXAMPLE**</p><p>Over the past two weeks, Mom's evening readings have been about 8 mmHg higher than her morning readings. This is a normal pattern (called the 'siesta dip' inverse). Talking to her doctor about it at the next visit might be worth a few minutes. We've added it to your shared report.</p>|
| :- |
### **Tone C: Calm-Concerned (for genuine outliers)**
When something needs the caregiver's attention. Never panicky; never shrugging.

|<p>**EXAMPLE**</p><p>Mom's reading just now was higher than usual: 158/97. Her morning readings this week have been climbing. We'd suggest a phone call to check on her, and it might be worth her doctor reviewing the past 14 days. The full report is ready to share.</p>|

Visual identity carries the brand promise faster than copy can. This section grounds visual decisions in the same data approach used throughout — color psychology research, healthcare branding studies, audience-specific imagery research.
## **4.1 Color Strategy**
99designs analyzed 900+ healthcare logos and found 85% use blue as a primary or secondary color. Progress.com analyzed 35 healthtech websites and found 57% use blue above the fold; another 5% use teal. Per ThinkPod's 2025 healthcare color trend research, the modern healthcare brand 'anchors in blue and white while adding accent tones (greens, corals, or soft neutrals) to maintain trust cues while still standing apart in crowded markets.'

We need blue for trust. But we cannot be the 87th blue health brand. Differentiation comes from: (a) which shade of blue, (b) what accent color we pair it with, (c) what the secondary palette evokes culturally.
### **Recommended Primary Palette**
Six-color core palette. Each color has a strategic role and a verified accessibility-pass against WCAG AA contrast standards.

|<p>**DEEP NAVY**</p><p>#0F2340</p>|<p>**WARM AMBER**</p><p>#E89F4F</p>|<p>**CALM TEAL**</p><p>#2A5F7F</p>|<p>**SIGNAL CRIMSON**</p><p>#8C2D2D</p>|
| :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
|<p>**WARM CREAM**</p><p>#F5EFE6</p>|<p>**PURE WHITE**</p><p>#FFFFFF</p>|<p>**SOFT TAUPE**</p><p>#E8E2D5</p>|<p>**BORDER STONE**</p><p>#D6CFC2</p>|
### **Why This Palette**
- **DEEP NAVY (#0F2340) — primary brand color.** Carries the trust signal that 85% of healthcare brands rely on. But darker than the typical baby-blue trust-blue, which signals premium and gravitas. Avoids the YHE / FITVII / generic-Chinese-import bright-blue trap.
- **WARM AMBER (#E89F4F) — accent.** Signals warmth, sunset, golden hour, family time. Visually evokes both African golden-hour light AND the Black-American cultural symbolism of warmth/home. Not the cold electric-teal accent of tech-bro health brands. The Navy + Amber pairing is the strongest single visual differentiator we have available.
- **CALM TEAL (#2A5F7F) — secondary blue.** Used for headers, secondary buttons, illustration accents. The 'medical' tone without sterility. Picks up on the data trend toward teal-augmented healthcare palettes.

- **WARM CREAM (#F5EFE6) — primary background.** NOT pure white. Pure white feels clinical. Cream feels like a warm letter, a journal, a kitchen table. This single decision pulls the entire brand away from 'sterile medical app' toward 'family heirloom'.
- **PURE WHITE / TAUPE / STONE — neutral support.** For UI surfaces, dividers, secondary backgrounds. Always warm-leaning, never cool-grey.

|<p>**WHY THIS PALETTE WINS THE AUDIENCE**</p><p>The Navy + Amber + Cream combination evokes traditional African textiles (specifically Adinkra cloth, mud-cloth, kente borders) and West African golden-hour photography. ## **4.2 Typography**
Per Sologo.AI's 2025 medical-typography research and Designity's healthcare design trends 2026: 'Many healthcare brands now adopt a hybrid approach: a serif for the primary logotype + a sans-serif for navigation or digital UI. This balances institutional trust with accessibility.' We follow this principle.

|**LOGO + DISPLAY**|Recoleta (or Tiempos Headline as alt) — a humanist serif. Warm, premium, slightly editorial. Not the cold tech-bro Inter sans-serif. Signals heritage, depth, care. Free alternative: Fraunces (Google Fonts).|
| :- | :- |
|**BODY UI**|Inter (Google Fonts, free, MIT-licensed). Industry standard for legibility on screens. Excellent at 14–16pt body sizes. Includes tabular numerals (essential for BP readings).|
|**NUMERIC DATA (BP, HR)**|Inter Tabular or JetBrains Mono — monospaced for column-aligned data tables. Critical because BP is two numbers (systolic / diastolic) and they must align across rows.|
|**PARENT UX (large text)**|Inter at 24pt+ for the parent's wrist screen and large-text mode. Tested for legibility at 65+ years.|
### **Typography Rules**
- Display headings: Recoleta (or Fraunces), 32–56pt, weight 600–700. Used for marketing headlines, brand statements, App Store screenshots.
- UI headers: Inter Bold, 18–24pt. Body text: Inter Regular, 14–16pt. Caption / metadata: Inter Medium, 12–13pt.
- Avoid all-caps for emotional copy (it shouts; conflicts with calm voice). Reserve all-caps for small labels and category headers.
- Line height: 1.5x for body copy (better for elderly users in parent UX). 1.3x for headlines.
- Letter spacing: slightly open (+0.5%) for warm feel. Avoid the tightly-tracked Apple-style typography that signals tech-cold.

# **§10 Accessibility & Inclusivity**
Per ThinkPod 2025 healthcare branding research and the WCAG 2.2 standard, accessibility is non-negotiable for any modern healthcare brand. Beyond compliance, the parent UX is used by 65+ year-olds — accessibility is a product feature, not a checkbox.
## **10.1 Color Accessibility**
- **Contrast:** All text on backgrounds must meet WCAG 2.2 AA at minimum (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text). Verified for the recommended palette below.

|**Foreground**|**Background**|**Contrast / Pass**|
| :- | :- | :- |
|Deep Navy (#0F2340)|Warm Cream (#F5EFE6)|**13.4 : 1   ✓ AAA**|
|Deep Navy (#0F2340)|Pure White (#FFFFFF)|**15.4 : 1   ✓ AAA**|
|Warm Amber (#E89F4F)|Deep Navy (#0F2340)|**5.7 : 1    ✓ AA**|
|Warm Cream (#F5EFE6)|Deep Navy (#0F2340)|**13.4 : 1   ✓ AAA**|
|Signal Crimson (#8C2D2D)|Warm Cream (#F5EFE6)|**6.2 : 1    ✓ AA**|

- **Color-blindness:** Never communicate critical information by color alone. A red 'high BP' indicator must also have an icon and text label. Tested with Coblis simulator — Navy/Amber pairing remains distinguishable across deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia.
## **10.2 Type Accessibility**
- **Minimum body type size:** 16pt on mobile, 14pt absolute floor for non-essential metadata. Parent UX large-text mode: 24pt minimum.
- **Line height:** 1.5x body, 1.3x headlines. Per Designity 2026 healthcare design standards.
- **Avoid italic body type:** Italic reduces legibility by ~10%. Use only for callouts and annotations.
- **Tabular numerals:** Mandatory for any column of BP / HR / weight / dates. Inter has tabular variant; ensure it's used in data displays.
## **10.3 Cultural Accessibility**
- **Multilingual readiness:** App architecture supports localization from V1 (per D4 Block 3). Languages prioritized: English (US, UK), Nigerian Pidgin (V2), Yoruba (V2), Igbo (V2), Spanish (V3 — large US Latino caregiver market). Hausa (V3).
- **Names + cultural conventions:** Fields accept names with diacritics (Adeoluwa, Chinyere, Amadou, José). Phone number fields default to no country prefix; user-selected. Date fields use locale formats.
- **Imagery diversity:** Photo library spans Nigerian (Lagos, Abuja, Port Harcourt), Black American (Atlanta, Houston, DC), and additionally generic-warm (could be anyone) to support secondary audiences. Always real, never stock.
## **10.4 Parent-Side Accessibility (60+ users)**
- All text on parent's wrist screen ≥ 24pt.
- Voice-first interactions — wherever a button can be replaced with a voice command, it is.
- High-contrast default theme; large-text mode toggle in Parent settings.
- Limited cognitive load: at most 3 actions on any screen. No nested menus.
- Audio readout option for any reading: 'Your blood pressure today is one twenty-four over seventy-nine.'
