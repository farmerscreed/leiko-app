Resting heart rate moves on two timescales — short and slow. Both show up in your watch trend; recognising which is which makes the chart easier to read.

## Short-timescale changes

Some things move resting heart rate within hours:

- Caffeine — a few extra beats per minute for one to two hours
- Stimulants in some prescribed medications
- Stress, anger, hurry — minutes
- Illness, especially with fever — days
- Dehydration after heat or hard movement — hours
- A heavy meal — usually thirty to ninety minutes
- Alcohol — six to twenty-four hours, depending on quantity

These show up as spikes on the trend chart, not as a new baseline. The line returns to its usual level within a day or two.

## Slow-timescale changes

Other things move the baseline gradually, across weeks or longer:

- Loss of fitness — heart rate climbs as the cardiovascular system has less work to do
- Gain of fitness — the opposite; a stronger heart at rest pumps more blood per beat and needs fewer beats
- Age — slow upward drift across decades
- Sleep quality — short or fragmented sleep, sustained, often shows up as a higher resting baseline
- Body weight changes
- Long-term changes in stress level

These show up as a gradual rise or fall in the line, not a single spike.

## A pattern worth noticing

A sustained rise in your resting heart rate — say, ten beats per minute over four to six weeks — without an obvious explanation is worth a mention at your next visit. The same pattern in the other direction is usually the reward of consistent sleep or movement.

Single-day spikes are noise. Trends are signal.

## Caffeine deserves its own line

Caffeine is the change most people notice on the watch and least often think about as a cause. If your morning resting heart rate looks unusually high, the question to ask is "what time did I take my first coffee yesterday and today?" Often the answer is the answer.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
