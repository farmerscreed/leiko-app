When a reading is at 180/120 mmHg or above, clinicians use two specific words to describe what happens next: urgency, and emergency. Both involve the same range of numbers; the difference is whether the body is signalling something more.

## Two clinical terms, one calm explanation

A hypertensive **urgency** is a reading at or above 180/120 mmHg without the kind of body signals that suggest the high pressure is causing damage right now. Most urgencies are managed by your doctor on a same-day call or visit, not in an emergency room.

A hypertensive **emergency** is a reading at or above 180/120 mmHg that comes with the urgent body signals your doctor has discussed with you. An emergency needs urgent care now — call your local emergency line or get to an emergency department directly.

Your doctor knows which signals matter for you. If you have not had that conversation, ask at your next visit. The right time to learn the difference is before you are deciding in the moment.

## What to do with a single very high reading on a calm morning

If the reading is high but you feel completely fine, the watch may have caught a measurement error or a moment of unusual stress. The right response is the same as for any high reading: sit, breathe slowly for several minutes, and re-take on the same arm. If the second reading is meaningfully lower, the first one was a moment.

If both readings sit in this range and you feel fine, call your doctor today — same day, not next week. They will tell you what to do next.

## When in doubt

If you are not sure whether the reading is an urgency or an emergency, default to calling your doctor or your local emergency line. They are there for exactly this question, and the cost of asking is small. The cost of waiting can be larger.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
