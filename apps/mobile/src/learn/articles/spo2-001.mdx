SpO2 is a number that estimates how much oxygen is being carried in your blood, shown as a percentage. Most healthy adults read between 95 and 100%.

## What the number is telling you

Your blood carries oxygen from your lungs to the rest of your body. <Definition term="SpO2">"S-P-O-2" — the percentage of oxygen-carrying spots on your red blood cells that are filled</Definition>. Higher is, generally, what the body wants.

Below 95%, the watch may be telling you one of two things: your blood oxygen is briefly lower than usual, or — more often — the sensor is not reading you well at that moment.

## What the watch can and cannot tell you

The SpO2 reading on your watch is a wellness reference. It is not a clinical measurement. Wrist-worn sensors are less accurate than the pulse oximeters a doctor uses, and the watch is built to give you a sense of the trend, not a precise number.

Several things make a wrist sensor read low when blood oxygen is fine:

- Cold hands or a tight band
- Tattoos or scars under the sensor
- Movement during the reading
- Darker skin tones — a known limitation of optical sensors
- A sensor that has slipped against the skin

If you have a chronic respiratory condition such as asthma, COPD, or sleep apnea, do not rely on the watch SpO2 for any decision about your care. Use the device your doctor recommends.

## What is worth sharing

A pattern of readings consistently in the low 90s across many days is worth mentioning at your next visit. A single low reading, especially with cold hands or after a busy moment, is common and usually means the sensor was not reading well.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
