Resting heart rate is how many times your heart beats in a minute when you are sitting still and not under stress. Most healthy adults sit somewhere between 60 and 100 beats per minute.

## What the watch is measuring

The watch samples your heart rate throughout the day. The "resting" number you see is the lower edge of that day's range — usually pulled from sitting periods or overnight. It is not a single reading; it is a quiet baseline.

For most adults, that baseline lands between 60 and 100. Athletes and very fit people often sit lower, in the 40s or 50s, because a stronger heart pushes more blood per beat and needs fewer beats to do the same work.

## What moves it over time

Some things raise your resting heart rate, sometimes for hours, sometimes for years:

- Caffeine and stimulants (short term)
- Stress, illness, fever, dehydration (short term)
- Certain prescribed medications (your doctor will know which)
- Loss of fitness over months
- Age, slowly, across decades

Other things tend to lower it:

- Consistent moderate movement, over weeks
- Better sleep, over weeks
- Recovery from an illness

If your resting heart rate trends up by ten beats over a few weeks without an obvious reason, it is worth a mention at your next visit.

## Heart rate and blood pressure are different stories

A high heart rate does not mean high blood pressure. A heart rate in range does not mean blood pressure is in range. The two are linked — both rise during exercise, both can rise under stress — but they tell different stories about your body. Your watch shows both because each adds context the other does not.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
