Of the cross-vital patterns the watch can show, the link between sleep and morning blood pressure is the strongest and most consistent. Watch the sleep chart and the BP chart side by side across two weeks; the pattern often appears clearly.

## The pattern

A short or fragmented night tends to lift the next morning's top number by a few mmHg over a person's usual baseline. Across a week of short nights, the lift compounds — the body has not had its usual maintenance window. Once a more-rested night returns, the morning numbers settle.

This is not an iron rule for any single person on any single morning. Plenty of nights are short for unrelated reasons and the next morning looks fine. The pattern shows up over weeks, not days.

## Why the body does this

Blood vessels relax during deep sleep. Hormones that regulate fluid balance and stress reset overnight. The body uses sleep for vascular maintenance the way it uses sleep for memory and tissue repair. When the maintenance window is short, the next morning often shows it.

## What the watch can see

A correlation in Leiko's view is not a single calculation; it is a pattern across enough nights and mornings to be more than chance. The app uses about two weeks of paired sleep and morning-BP readings before showing a meaningful correlation strength. Below that, the line is too noisy to call.

If the correlation is showing in your chart, that is real information you can take to your doctor. If it isn't showing, that does not mean sleep and BP are unrelated for you — it usually means the watch needs more weeks of paired readings.

## A useful frame for the conversation

When a doctor and a person sit down to talk about morning numbers, "I sleep five to six hours most weeknights and my morning numbers are higher then" is a much richer starting point than "my numbers are sometimes high." The first is a pattern. The second is a worry.

The trend chart turns the worry into the pattern. That is most of what it is for.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
