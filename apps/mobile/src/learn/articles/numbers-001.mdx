A reading like <Reading sys={124} dia={79} /> is two numbers — the pressure your blood is making against the inside of your arteries, taken at one moment.

## Two numbers, one reading

The top number is <Definition term="systolic">the pressure when your heart contracts and pushes blood out</Definition>. The bottom is <Definition term="diastolic">the pressure between beats, when the heart fills again</Definition>. Both are measured in mmHg — millimetres of mercury, a leftover unit from old glass-tube cuffs that the medical world never replaced.

Both numbers matter. A reading is in the higher range if either number is high.

## What "in range" looks like

The AHA and ACC, who set the most-followed guideline in the United States, call below 120/80 mmHg the in-range mark for adults. Above that, readings step up through tiers — elevated, then Stage 1, then Stage 2. Other guidelines, including the European ones and NICE in the UK, draw the line at 140/90 instead. Your doctor will use whichever the practice follows.

A reading is a snapshot. One reading does not define you, and the watch on your wrist has not made a clinical judgement about your health.

## One reading, many causes

Blood pressure shifts through the day. A 20 mmHg swing in the top number across a single day is common. Things that nudge it up or down:

- Food, especially salty meals
- Sleep — short or restless sleep often raises morning numbers
- Caffeine, sometimes for an hour or two
- Stress and conversation, even mild
- A full bladder, a cold cuff, sitting cross-legged
- Recent activity

Two readings taken a minute apart can differ by 5–10 mmHg. The trend across days and weeks is more meaningful than any single number.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
