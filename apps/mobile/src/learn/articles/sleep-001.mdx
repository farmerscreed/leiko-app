Your watch estimates how you slept by watching your movement and heart rate through the night. It separates the night into four phases — awake, light, deep, and REM — and gives you a rough breakdown of how much time you spent in each.

## The four phases

**Awake** is exactly that — moments when you stirred, rolled, or briefly woke. A few short awake periods every night is part of how most adults sleep.

**Light** is most of your night. The body is still, the heart rate is low and steady, and you would wake easily from a small sound. Most adults spend about half their night here.

**Deep** is the heaviest sleep, often clustered earlier in the night. Heart rate drops further; movement is minimal. This is where the body does its slowest, deepest restoration work. Most adults get fifteen to twenty percent of their night here, and that share decreases gradually with age.

**REM** is when most dreaming happens. Heart rate becomes variable, the eyes move under the lids, and longer REM stretches usually come in the second half of the night. About a fifth of a typical night.

## Why this is an estimate

The clinical version of sleep tracking is a polysomnography — sensors on the scalp, chest, and legs in a sleep lab. The watch does none of that. It uses heart-rate patterns and your movement to make an educated guess at the stages.

That guess is good enough for trends. It is not good enough to tell you anything about a sleep disorder. If you suspect you have one, your doctor has the right tests.

## What is worth watching

Total time asleep is the most useful thing the watch tracks. Number of awake periods is a good trend over weeks. The exact deep-sleep percentage on a single night is not — it varies enough night-to-night that one number, in isolation, does not mean much.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
