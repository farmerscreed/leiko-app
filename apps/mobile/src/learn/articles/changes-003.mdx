A reading taken right after a meal is rarely the most useful one. The body is busy: blood is moving toward the digestive system, the heart rate has shifted, and the autonomic system is doing several things at once. The reading reflects the moment, not the trend.

## Most adults see a small rise

For most people, blood pressure climbs slightly after a meal — especially a large one, a salty one, or one washed down with caffeine. The rise is usually 5 to 10 mmHg in the top number and lasts an hour or two. Then the reading settles.

This is part of why home BP guidance recommends taking readings either before a meal or about an hour after. A reading thirty minutes after a heavy lunch is not "wrong" — it just is not directly comparable to a morning reading on an empty stomach.

## Some older adults see the opposite pattern

A smaller share of older adults experience the reverse: blood pressure drops noticeably after a meal. The clinical name is postprandial hypotension. It is less talked about than the rise, but it is real, and it can leave a person feeling lightheaded for an hour after eating. If you notice that pattern in your readings, mention it at your next visit. Your doctor can suggest small changes — smaller meals more often, less alcohol with meals, more time before standing — that often help.

## What the trend looks like

If you log readings around the same time of day, before meals, the chart shows the steady picture. If your readings drift around meal times, the chart will be noisier and harder to read week to week. Pick a time and stick with it. Most people choose a morning routine before the first meal — that is when the body is in its most predictable state.

A higher post-meal reading does not mean the meal "caused" hypertension. It means the body is doing what bodies do.

Talk to your doctor about what is right for you.
